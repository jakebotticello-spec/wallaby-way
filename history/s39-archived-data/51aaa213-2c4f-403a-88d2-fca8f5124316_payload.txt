Quick 3D printing question about PETG transparency and line width.
Thicker lines. Wider extrusion paths mean fewer seams/boundaries between lines, which reduces the light-scattering interfaces inside the wall. Each boundary between adjacent lines acts like a tiny refractive disruption, so the fewer of those you have, the clearer the part looks.

A few practical tips: slow down your print speed to help the lines fully fuse together, bump up your hotend temp a bit (within PETG range), and use fewer perimeters with wider width rather than more perimeters at narrow width. Vase mode is the gold standard for transparency since it's literally one continuous line with zero seams.
===END===

That's a solid plan. The 0.5mm bottom shell (two layers at .25 or three at .16) will give you that translucent diffuser effect, and the sparse infill behind it adds rigidity without blocking much light. Basically a built-in lightbox backing.

A couple things to think about — for the infill pattern, go with grid or lines over gyroid/cubic. The geometric patterns will cast cleaner shadows if any light hits at an angle, and they're more predictable structurally in a flat panel. Something like 10-15% should give you enough rigidity to not warp or bow without killing the light transmission.

Also worth considering: at 3mm total with only 0.5mm solid bottom, that's 2.5mm of exposed infill on the back face. Totally fine functionally, just might snag on things. If that bugs you, even one top layer would clean it up without meaningfully blocking light.

For bottom layer pattern on a translucent PETG picture frame backing where light transmission matters - monotonic is the move. It gives the most uniform, consistent fill with minimal gaps/overlaps, which means more even light diffusion across the surface.
Monotonic. It fills in one direction consistently so you get even light diffusion with no overlap lines or artifacts. The shaped patterns (concentric, Archimedean, etc.) will create visible patterns in the light that would show through behind your picture. Monotonic keeps it clean and uniform.
