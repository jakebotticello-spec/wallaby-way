 Totally fair — seams on a hero piece are a real aesthetic hit, and on a faceted low-poly model they're actually *harder* to hide than on an organic smooth model because the flat planes make any misalignment catch the light.

Honest answer: **no, don't bisect him if seams bother you.** The juice isn't worth the squeeze here. A clean single print with visible support scars on the wing undersides (which nobody sees on a display piece) beats a split print with a seam running down his chest that you'll notice every time you walk past.

Here's what I'd actually do instead:

**Print him upright, as the model was designed.** The sculptor oriented him standing for a reason — the feet are the intended ground contact, the pose reads correctly, and the "hero" surfaces (face, chest, wing tops) all print as upward-facing faces with great detail.

**Use tree supports (organic)** in Bambu Studio. The organic tree supports are *dramatically* better than the old tree style — they snake up to exactly where they're needed, touch the model at tiny points, and peel off with minimal scarring. For wing undersides specifically, they're the right tool.

Settings that matter:
- Support type: **tree (organic)**
- Support/object XY distance: 0.35mm
- Top Z distance: 0.2mm
- Interface layers: 2
- Interface pattern: rectilinear interlaced
- Support on build plate only: **off** (let them grow from the model where needed — under wings specifically)

**Where supports will land:**
- Wing undersides (big one — unavoidable)
- Under the beak/chin
- Possibly under the tail feathers if they flare out
- Maybe the underside of the "shoulder" where wings meet body

**The wing-underside scarring question** — here's the thing: on a display piece sitting on a shelf or desk, you almost never see the undersides of the wings. Eye level is usually at or below the phoenix, so you're looking at the chest, face, and wing *tops*. The scarred surfaces face down and away. This is exactly the scenario tree supports were designed for.

**Brim** — 5mm brim, those feet are small contact patches and this is a tall print. You don't want a layer shift or a knock-over at hour 28.

**One more thought on the AMS:** with him upright, your color transitions happen layer-by-layer going up. The gold on the beak and feet means the gold filament only gets loaded for the feet layers (early) and the beak layers (middle), not constantly throughout. That's actually *efficient* — less purge waste than you'd think.

Go upright, tree organic supports, matte PLA, and trust that the undersides won't show. He'll look great.

 Ha — good catch, because fuzzy skin changes the math on #2 significantly.

**Without fuzzy skin:** 0.28mm is a no-brainer on this model. The facets are so much larger than the layer lines that you genuinely can't tell 0.20 from 0.28 on a faceted surface. Free 25% time savings.

**With fuzzy skin:** different story. Fuzzy skin works by jittering the outer wall in/out on each layer — the "fuzz" texture *is* made of layer lines. At 0.28mm layer height, each fuzz bump is taller and coarser, and you get a chunkier, more granular texture instead of the fine feather-like fuzz you probably want on a phoenix. It ends up looking more like stucco than plumage.

For fuzzy skin specifically:
- **0.20mm** = fine, feathery, organic texture — what you want
- **0.16mm** = even better, genuinely feather-like, but adds time back
- **0.28mm** = coarse, pebbly, fights against the "soft" look fuzzy skin is for

**So the real question is: is fuzzy skin worth it on this model?**

My take — **yes, but selectively.** Faceted low-poly models are geometric and hard-edged by design. Adding fuzzy skin to the *entire* model softens and muddies the facets, which is fighting the whole aesthetic. You'd lose the crisp triangle definition that makes low-poly look good.

But fuzzy skin **on the wings and tail only**, with body/head left smooth, could look incredible — suggests feather texture exactly where feathers should be, keeps the sculptural facets sharp on the body and face. Bambu Studio lets you paint fuzzy skin on specific faces via the "Fuzzy Skin" painter.

**Recommendation:**
- If you're doing fuzzy skin on wings/tail: **stay at 0.20mm**, accept the time hit (back to ~20h at 80% scale single color)
- If no fuzzy skin: **go 0.28mm**, keep the time savings

I'd actually lean toward no fuzzy skin on this one. The sculpt is clearly designed around clean faceted planes — the artist wanted geometric, not organic. Fuzzy skin on the wings would be cool but it's adding a texture the sculptor didn't intend. 0.28mm, smooth, single color, paint the accents. ~10 hours. Done.

 Okay, real talk on fuzzy skin for this specific model:

**The case FOR it:**
- Wings and tail are the biggest surfaces and reading them as "feathers" sells the phoenix illusion
- Low-poly can look a little "video game asset" unaltered — fuzzy skin adds tactile/organic quality that photographs and displays well
- The P1S does fuzzy skin really well, it's a solved feature at this point
- Hides layer lines on near-vertical surfaces, which helps at 0.28mm layer height

**The case AGAINST it:**
- Fights the sculptor's intent — this model is clearly designed around clean geometric facets, fuzz softens those edges
- On the body/chest facets (which are meant to read as hard planes), fuzzy skin makes them look like poorly-printed smooth surfaces rather than intentional geometry
- Adds print time (outer wall has to jitter in/out on every layer — maybe +10-15%)
- Can look weird on faces/beaks where you want crisp definition

**My recommendation: selective fuzzy skin via paint-on in Bambu Studio.**

Open the fuzzy skin painter and apply it only to:
- ✅ Wing feathers (both sides)
- ✅ Tail feathers
- ✅ Chest "feather ruff" area if the model has one
- ❌ Face / beak / head — keep crisp
- ❌ Body facets (the big geometric planes on the torso)
- ❌ Feet — keep clean for painting

**Settings if you go for it:**
- Fuzzy skin thickness: **0.3mm** (default is often 0.3, good starting point)
- Fuzzy skin point distance: **0.75mm** (tighter = finer texture)
- Fuzzy skin first layer: **off** (keep the base clean)

**One gotcha at 0.28mm layer height:** fuzz quality is *okay* but not amazing. If you're committing to fuzzy skin as a hero feature, drop back to 0.20mm for the feather areas and accept the ~3-hour time hit. If you're just adding subtle texture, 0.28mm is fine.

**Honest gut check:** for THIS phoenix, I'd probably **skip it.** The facets are the design language — adding fuzz is like adding woodgrain texture to an Art Deco sculpture. It's not wrong, but it's muddying what makes it look good. Let the low-poly be low-poly. If you want feathers, the *next* phoenix you print should be a realistic sculpt where fuzzy skin earns its keep.

 Good call committing to a direction. At 0.28mm layer height the fuzz is going to be chunkier by nature, so you want to tune it to work *with* that coarseness instead of fighting it. Here's what I'd punch in:

**Fuzzy Skin settings at 0.28mm:**

- **Fuzzy skin:** All walls (not "Outer wall only" — at 0.28mm you want the displacement to have structural depth, not just a wiggly outer line)
  - *Actually scratch that — stick with "Outer wall only" unless you see weirdness. All walls is overkill and adds time. Outer wall is standard.*
- **Fuzzy skin thickness:** **0.4mm** (default 0.3 gets lost at chunky layer heights — bump it up for visibility)
- **Fuzzy skin point distance:** **1.0mm** (default 0.75 is fine, but at 0.28mm layers the jitter frequency should match the coarser look — 1.0mm spacing reads as intentional texture instead of printer noise)
- **Fuzzy skin first layer:** Off (clean base, better adhesion)

**Why these values:**
At 0.28mm, each "fuzz bump" is a 0.28mm-tall ring of displacement. Default settings (0.3mm thickness, 0.75mm distance) are tuned for 0.20mm layers where the bumps are finer and closer. Scaling both values up ~30% keeps the visual rhythm matched to the layer height — you get texture that looks *designed* rather than artifacted.

**Wall/speed settings to pair with it:**

- **Outer wall speed:** Drop to **~150mm/s** (or whatever your profile has, then knock it down 20%). Fuzzy skin does a ton of tiny direction changes — faster speeds = ringing/ghosting in the fuzz pattern
- **Outer wall acceleration:** Lower is better here too. If your profile has outer wall accel, try 3000-5000 mm/s²
- **Walls:** Keep at 2, maybe 3 if you want more structural integrity behind the fuzz (the jittering outer wall is slightly weaker than smooth)

**Paint-on coverage reminder:**

In the Fuzzy Skin painter:
- Brush size: 5-8mm for large areas (wing surfaces)
- Smaller brush (2-3mm) when you're near the boundaries with smooth areas — you want clean transitions, not feathered edges (pun intended)
- Apply to both top and bottom of each wing
- Tail feathers: full coverage
- Feather edges where they meet the body: taper off, don't hard-stop at an edge if possible

**Time impact heads up:**
Expect your 13h11m to become something like **14h30m - 15h30m** with fuzzy skin on the wings/tail only. Outer wall is the slowest wall, and fuzzy skin makes it slower. Not a dealbreaker, still a one-overnight print.

**Final sanity check before you slice:**
Run a slice, then scrub to a mid-height layer and **zoom way in on a fuzzy wing surface in the preview.** You're looking for:
- Fuzz bumps visible and roughly uniform
- Not so dense it reads as "noise"
- Not so sparse it reads as "printer malfunction"
- Clean transition where fuzz meets smooth body
