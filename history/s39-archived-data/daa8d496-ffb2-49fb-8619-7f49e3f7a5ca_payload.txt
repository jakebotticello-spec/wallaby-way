Hey Jake. Short answer: yes, but the optimization mostly happens on *their* side, not yours.

Your side is dead simple. If they're a non-US person doing the work outside the US, you don't withhold anything. You collect a **W-8BEN** (or W-8BEN-E if they're an entity), pay the invoice, and you don't even file a 1099 — foreign contractors aren't 1099 reportable. That keeps your hands clean.

The "creative but legal" part is what *they* do on their end. A few common moves contractors use:

- **Invoice as a registered business, not an individual.** Most countries tax corporate or sole-proprietor income at lower effective rates than W-2-equivalent personal income, especially after expense deductions.
- **Use a tax-favorable jurisdiction if they're flexible.** Estonia (e-Residency + OÜ — 0% on retained earnings), UAE (0% personal), Georgia (1% small business regime up to ~$200K), Paraguay, Cyprus, etc. Some clients literally relocate for this.
- **Territorial tax countries.** Places like Panama, Costa Rica, Malaysia don't tax foreign-source income at all, so if you're paying them from a US entity, that income often isn't taxed locally.
- **Treaty benefits.** If their country has a US tax treaty, the W-8BEN claims it and avoids double taxation.

What I'd push back on: don't get cute with calling it a "gift," routing through a third party, or paying to a different name than who's doing the work. That's where legal becomes evasion fast, and it's *their* exposure but it can splash on you if it ever unwinds.
