 That's a real project, but it's doable. A few things worth thinking through before you commit:

**Length and bends are the killers on portable AC exhaust.** These units are designed for a 4-6 foot run straight out a window. Every foot of length and every bend adds backpressure, which makes the compressor work harder, drops cooling capacity, and shortens the unit's life. A run up through the ceiling, across the crawl space (attic, I assume you mean?), and out the soffit is probably 15-25 feet with at least two 90° bends. You'll lose meaningful cooling capacity and the unit will run nearly constantly.

**Mitigations that actually help:**
- Upsize the duct. Most portables come with 5" or 6" hose. Going to 8" rigid insulated duct for the long horizontal run reduces backpressure significantly. Use the stock hose only for the flexible connection at the unit itself.
- Insulate the whole run. That exhaust air is 130-140°F. If it's running through your attic uninsulated, you're dumping heat directly above your ceiling and the attic radiates it back down. Insulated flex duct or wrapping rigid duct in R-8 makes a real difference.
- Keep the run as straight as possible and use long-radius elbows, not sharp 90s.
- Soffit termination needs a proper vented cap with a backdraft damper, otherwise hot attic air flows back into the unit when it cycles off.

**The bigger issue nobody mentions with single-hose portables:** they exhaust air out of the room, which creates negative pressure, which pulls hot unconditioned air in through every gap in the room. They're roughly half as efficient as a window unit for this reason. If you can find a dual-hose portable (one intake, one exhaust), it's dramatically better — no negative pressure, no infiltration. Worth checking what you've got before building infrastructure around it.

**Honest alternative to consider:** a through-the-wall sleeve unit. Cutting a 16" × 26" hole in an exterior wall sounds scarier than it is — between studs, no header needed for that size, sleeve kit handles the flashing and trim. You'd have a permanent, properly-sized AC that cools way better than any portable, and you don't lose the sliding door or run ductwork through the attic. Probably a weekend project and runs $400-600 for a decent 12-14k BTU unit plus the sleeve.

What's the BTU on the portable you've got sitting around? And single or dual hose?

 Now you're talking. This is a really good 3D print problem, actually — small functional parts, no exotic geometry, you'd print it once and use it for years.

**The basic concept that makes sense:**

A vertical extrusion (printed in segments since your P1S build volume maxes at ~256mm tall) that sits in the slider track, runs floor to header, and presents a flat face toward the closed slider. The slider closes against it like it's the jamb. You build in:

- A 5-6" round port at portable AC hose height (typically 30-40" off the floor) with a removable cap for winter
- A foam or TPU gasket strip on the slider-facing side for a real seal
- Slot or channel for the slider's existing weatherstrip to mate into
- Bottom foot that locks into the track without modifying it
- Top piece that wedges or cams against the header

**Build approach:**

Print in 200-250mm tall segments with dovetail or screw-together joins. PETG is the right call here — UV stable enough for door-adjacent use, dimensionally stable across temperature swings, and you've got the PETG-CF in stock if you want stiffness. Straight PETG is probably fine since the slider is doing the structural work of holding it in place.

For the seal face, print a channel and press in adhesive-backed foam weatherstripping (the closed-cell EPDM stuff, not the open-cell foam crap). Or print a TPU gasket strip — you've got TPU on hand — though foam tape is honestly easier and replaceable.

**The port detail matters.** A flat hose flange will leak. Print a short stub that the hose clamps onto, with a 5° taper if you're using the typical clockwise-threaded portable AC hose end so it self-tightens when twisted on. The cap for the off-season can just be a press-fit plug with a finger pull.

**Things to think through before designing:**

- Slider track width and depth (measure both top and bottom — they're sometimes different)
- Whether your slider has a hook latch or a deadbolt-style lock, and whether it can engage against a printed surface or needs a metal strike plate embedded
- Header height (full opening) so you know how many segments
- Hose diameter on your specific portable AC — measure, don't assume
- Whether the slider closes flush or has any gap that needs to be bridged

If you measure those up and want to start designing, I can put together an OpenSCAD parametric file for it. Lots of variables to dial in but it's the kind of thing that benefits from being parametric since you'll probably tweak it after the first test fit.

Air-only gap is decent but convection inside the cavity will move heat across it. Two options if you want to actually kill the heat transfer:

- Stuff the cavity with fiberglass batt scrap or rockwool after assembly — kills convection, R-value jumps significantly, costs nothing if you have any insulation lying around
- Print internal baffles every 100-150mm vertically to break up convection cells. Easy to add to the model, no extra material to source

Honestly for a summer-only install where the exhaust hose is the actual heat source you're worried about (140°F air inside a duct touching the inner wall), I'd just insulate the *hose* with a foil-faced duct wrap and not worry as much about the panel walls themselves. The panel only needs to keep outside air out and inside air in — the hose is what's actively radiating.

**On the white/off-white PETG:**

Worth knowing before you order: white PETG shows print lines and layer artifacts more than almost any other color. Black hides everything, white shows everything. If aesthetics matter, lean toward a matte or "silk" white rather than glossy, and bump your top/bottom layer count to 6-7. Polymaker PolyLite PETG in white is reliable and pretty matte. Overture white PETG is cheaper and fine. Esun is hit or miss on white specifically — sometimes the pigment load is uneven.

Off-white / cream / "natural" PETG is sometimes easier to get a clean finish on than bright white, if that fits the room.

**On the bug mesh:**

Smart. Print a recessed frame on the inner face of the port with a lip, and either glue/sandwich a square of fiberglass window screen in there, or design it as two rings that screw together to clamp the mesh. The second option lets you replace the screen if it ever clogs with dust.

**Remaining things to actually measure:**

- Track width top and bottom (they often differ by a few mm)
- Track depth top and bottom (the top track is usually deeper so the panel can be lifted up into it and dropped down into the bottom — that's how it goes in)
- Total opening height floor-to-top-of-bottom-track + bottom-of-top-track
- Slider panel thickness (so the foam seal stands proud the right amount)
- Hose OD on the AC

Get me those numbers and the segment height you want (I'd suggest 240mm to leave headroom under the 256mm Z), and I can write the OpenSCAD. - Lug height of 4-5mm, width of 8-10mm
- A shallow detent at the locked position so it doesn't rotate back loose
- A small printed tab or wing on the port for hand-tightening grip

A check valve to prevent backflow when the AC cycles off would actually be useful — a simple hinged flap inside the port that the exhaust pushes open and gravity/light spring closes when flow stops.  Ah, got it — you're describing a diffuser, not a venturi. And yeah, that's actually fine, just for different reasons than negative pressure.

What you're describing: small inlet (matched to AC hose), expanding to a larger diameter as it passes through the panel. The exhaust air slows down as it expands into the larger cross-section, which:

- Reduces exhaust velocity at the outlet (less wind noise, less pressure differential at the panel-to-outside interface)
- Slightly reduces backpressure on the AC compared to a straight-through port at the smaller diameter, because the air has somewhere to expand into
- Doesn't create suction or negative pressure — that part doesn't happen — but it doesn't *add* backpressure either, which is what I was worried about

Net effect is mildly positive for AC efficiency and meaningfully positive for noise. Not nothing.

**Geometry to actually do it right:**

Keep the expansion angle gradual — under 7° of half-angle (so under 14° total cone angle). Steeper than that and the airflow separates from the wall, you get turbulence, and you lose the benefit. For a 5" inlet expanding to, say, 7" over the panel thickness, you'd want at least 40-50mm of axial length to keep the cone shallow enough.

If your panel is thinner than that, you've got two options:

- Just use a straight-through port at the AC hose diameter and skip the diffuser — totally fine
- Make the port stick out past the panel face on the outside, giving the cone room to expand. Adds a printed nub on the exterior but keeps the geometry right

The bayonet mount still works the same way — the lugs sit on the inlet (small) end where it meets the panel, the cone expands outward toward the exterior side.

Worth doing? Marginal but not zero. If the print is easy to design either way, might as well.
